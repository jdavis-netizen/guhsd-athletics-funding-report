Executive Summary

The headline finding

GUHSD is not resource-constrained. It is the third-highest per-student-spending high school district in San Diego County, and holds general fund reserves roughly four times the state mandate. What is constrained is the share of those resources reaching athletics — and the gap is being closed by families and student ASBs, not by the district.

Five findings, one argument

  1. Per-student spending: GUHSD spends $21,644 per ADA — above Sweetwater, San Dieguito, Oceanside, San Marcos, and Poway. Peer districts spending up to 28% less per student fund athletics more generously than GUHSD.
  2. Athletic trainer staffing: GUHSD pays its AT as a $22,500 certificated stipend. Six of seven verified SD County peers — including districts spending 28% less per ADA — employ the AT as a full-time classified healthcare role with benefits and BOC/NATA certification required.
  3. Coaching stipend structure: Helix Charter (in the Grossmont League) pays head varsity football $1,125–$1,303 more than GUHSD at every experience tier. Sweetwater pays 40% more at entry level. GUHSD's stipend cap of 52 positions per school for 24+ sports forces programs to fundraise for any additional coaches.
  4. Athletic Director compensation: GUHSD's AD tops out at $6,227 — below SDUHSD ($7,354), Helix Charter combined AD+Asst AD ($10,216), and Steele Canyon Charter ($10,802). Steele Canyon's AD earns +73% more than GUHSD's with the same 2 release periods. Only Sweetwater's flat $4,256 AD stipend sits below GUHSD's top tier.
  5. Officials funding: GUHSD allocates $22,000 per school. At Granite Hills (verified FY 2025-26), actual competition overhead — Arbiter-paid officials plus tournament entry fees — totaled $57,393. The district allocation covered ~38% of that at one site alone; the remaining $35,393 was absorbed by sport-specific trust accounts funded through ASB and booster fundraising. Scaled across 9 comprehensive sites, the district-wide ASB burden is estimated at $162K–$297K per year.

The reframe

This brief does not argue the district lacks money. It argues the district has above-peer per-ADA funding, above-mandate reserves, and below-peer athletics allocation — and that the resulting gap is pushed onto student ASBs and family-led booster fundraising in a way that is not happening at peer districts with less to work with.

The charter competition question

Two of GUHSD's own conversion charters — Helix Charter HS (La Mesa) and Steele Canyon Charter HS (Spring Valley) — operate inside the Grossmont League and compete directly for families who would otherwise attend GUHSD comprehensive sites. Both charters' athletic compensation structures were verified for this brief:

  • Helix Charter pays head varsity football $8,849 at top tier — $1,238 more than GUHSD ($7,611). Helix's underlying teacher base salary is 16.3% higher than GUHSD's (Class 3 Step 1: $80,444 vs. $69,187).
  • Steele Canyon Charter uses an identical percentage formula to GUHSD's and explicitly credits GUHSD experience as equivalent on hire — but treats its Athletic Director as a senior administrative role, topping at $10,802 (+73% over GUHSD's $6,227) with two release periods per year versus GUHSD's one.

The retention lens

In a declining-enrollment environment where each lost student costs the district Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) dollars, athletics is one of the few direct levers a district controls for student engagement and attendance. Research consistently identifies athletics as a primary retention factor for charter school recruitment. When GUHSD's neighboring conversion charters invest more per coach and per AD than the district they were converted from, the long-run cost of underfunded athletics is not paid in stipends. It is paid in ADA.

The reserves context

GUHSD announced 61 layoff notices in February 2025 (49 credentialed, 12 classified, plus closure of the child care center) citing a $2.5 million deficit driven in part by declining enrollment. At the same time, per public reporting, the district maintains $30–45 million in general fund reserves — approximately four times the state-mandated reserve level. For context, the estimated district-wide ASB burden for officials (~$162,000–$297,000 per year, scaled from verified Granite Hills data) represents well under 1% of the district's reserves.

"If we can't save all the jobs, I want the absolute lowest number of cuts that we can possibly do." — GUHSD Board member Chris Fite, quoted in Voice of San Diego, March 2025.

The "GUHSD experience transfers" clause — verbatim from HTA Contract §13.1.4.1

"Coaches shall be credited with a maximum of 7 years outside coaching experience in other districts or the equivalent in the verified sport to be coached. All experience within the Grossmont Union High School district in the sport to be coached shall be credited to the members."

Practical effect: a 10+-year GUHSD head varsity coach moving to Helix lands at the top of Helix's schedule immediately on hire — no "start over" penalty. The retention threat, quantified

Helix enrolls 2,571 students (2025–26 CDE). Every year, some fraction of GUHSD's coaching staff reaches the career point where moving 15 minutes down the road to Helix means a $1,600–$2,500 annual raise plus an expanded stipend pool. Because Helix explicitly honors GUHSD experience, the transition cost to the coach is zero. For GUHSD, the cost is whatever that coach's program produces — and in many cases, the students who follow.

Regulatory context

California is the only U.S. state without athletic-trainer licensure. AB 796 (Chapter 899, Statutes of 2024) — signed by Governor Newsom on September 29, 2024 — established title protection only, not licensure. It makes it unlawful to hold oneself out as "athletic trainer," "certified athletic trainer," or similar without CAATE-program graduation and BOC certification, but does not require schools to employ ATs.

Peer-reviewed 2022–23 ATLAS survey data (Korey Stringer Institute / NATA, Journal of Athletic Training, 2024) found:

A $22,500 stipend model is not consistent with the compensation level required to recruit or retain a BOC-certified AT as a dedicated healthcare professional. This is the compensation structure that the ATLAS survey captures as "unqualified personnel in the AT role."

Peer-reviewed safety outcomes: schools without ATs have materially higher injury rates

The most directly comparative peer-reviewed study — Pierpoint et al., "Injuries in girls' soccer and basketball: a comparison of high schools with and without athletic trainers," Injury Epidemiology 5:29, 2018 — provides the empirical link between AT staffing and student safety:

Companion study (Journal of Athletic Training, 715 HS athletes): concussion-knowledge scores significantly higher with AT access (p ≤ .001). Memory-loss symptom recognition: 83% w/AT vs. 68% w/o. Sleep-problem recognition: 54% vs. 35%. Cognitive fogginess: 66% vs. 37%.

Direct implication for the 4 GUHSD walk-on-trainer sites

El Cajon Valley, Mount Miguel, El Capitan, and Santana currently rely on "walk-on" trainers rather than a dedicated certified AT. Per the Pierpoint rate ratios, students at these four sites are at materially higher statistical risk of recurrent injury than students at GUHSD's five CTE-teacher-AT sites. Both groups, in turn, are below the staffing standard (full-time classified BOC-certified AT) that peer districts SDUHSD and Oceanside USD maintain.

Title IX compliance exposure — the Morgan Hill precedent (October 2023)

In October 2023, the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) resolved a Title IX investigation into Morgan Hill Unified School District (Live Oak HS) that is directly analogous to GUHSD's current booster-funded athletics model.

OCR's findings at Live Oak HS:

  • Girls' programs "may not have had similar quality equipment, equal access to the school's weight room and athletic trainer, equal access to the school's athletic locker room, or equitable publicity"
  • Booster funding described by a Live Oak coach as "the wild west" — teams "fend for themselves" for funding
  • OCR found disparate benefits (equipment, facilities, AT access) between boys' and girls' programs

Resolution agreement — district commitments:

  • "Create committees to ensure that differences in fundraising between teams will not create disparities in athletic equipment, uniforms or supplies based on sex"
  • "Ensure the school's girls' and boys' athletics teams have equitable access to the school's athletic trainer, weight room, and training facilities"
"If booster clubs provide benefits that create disparities based on gender, the school is responsible for offsetting those disparate benefits. The origins of the money that created the inequities does not serve as a defense for the district and does not mitigate the obligation to remedy those inequities." — OCR / NFHS Title IX guidance

The exposure at Granite Hills

Granite Hills FY 2025-26 verified trust account spend: Football $85,219, Wrestling $41,549 — while Girls Basketball spent $15,349 and Girls Soccer $14,529. Boys sports (55.3%) outspent girls sports (36.2%) by roughly $67,000 at one site in one year — largely a function of booster fundraising capacity by sport, not a district-driven allocation. Under Morgan Hill, the fact that these disparities originate in booster fundraising does not relieve GUHSD of the Title IX obligation to ensure equivalent benefits. The specific equitable-access item OCR named at Live Oak — the school's athletic trainer — is the exact resource GUHSD currently funds via a $22,500 stipend and staffs via walk-ons at 4 of 9 sites.

Why the gap keeps widening

Post-COVID shortages of CIF officials have driven per-game rates up. The CIF Southern Section (the best-documented California benchmark) adopted a rate structure targeting $35/hour for varsity assignments. Current per-game rates for 2024–25:

The CIF–San Diego Section publishes its own 2024–26 fee sheet — rates are expected to be comparable. As rates rise, a fixed $22,000 per-school allocation covers a shrinking percentage of actual cost each year.

The scale matters — but not the way it first looks

An estimated $162K–$297K in district-wide ASB burden for competition overhead is 0.05–0.10% of GUHSD's $321M general fund — a rounding error at the district level. At the same time, it represents well under 1% of GUHSD's $30–45M reserves. The question this presents to the board is not whether the district can absorb this cost — it can — but whether it is strategically sound to keep pushing an operational athletics cost onto ASB and families when the district's own fiscal documents show ample room.

The comparison to recent district action

In February 2025, GUHSD issued 61 layoff notices citing a $2.5 million deficit. The full athletics proposal package costs $1.4 million — roughly 56% of that same deficit figure, and less than 5% of the district's reserves. Funding the entire package in year one would leave GUHSD with reserves still well above the state-mandated minimum, and aligned with every documented San Diego County peer district on athletics support.

Recommendations

Five specific, actionable asks

Each recommendation is grounded in a verified finding from this brief and is benchmarked against at least one San Diego County peer district's existing practice.

1
Convert the Athletic Trainer role from stipend to full-time classified position
Match the SDUHSD and Oceanside models — full-time classified, BOC/NATA certification required, CalPERS-eligible, with benefits. This brings GUHSD in line with 6 of 7 verified SD County peers and addresses the documented California-wide AT shortage captured in the 2022–23 ATLAS data.
2
Raise per-school officials allocation toward actual cost
Move the $22,000 per-school allocation toward actual competition overhead (Granite Hills verified at $57,393 for FY 2025-26), with automatic annual adjustments tied to published CIF–SDS rate increases. Even raising the per-school allocation to $40,000 would close roughly half the current ASB burden. This is well under 0.1% of the general fund.
3
Add named assistant coach stipend lines for high-staff sports
Follow Sweetwater's and SMUSD's model: add specific dollar-figured stipend lines for Assistant Football (mirror Sweetwater's $5,254), Assistant Wrestling, Assistant Swimming, and Assistant Track, in addition to the existing Class II generic assistant tier. Retire the 52-position per-school cap in favor of sport-specific allocations.
4
Reclassify the Athletic Director stipend
Move the AD off the JV/Assistant schedule line (Class III equivalent) and onto a weighted administrative tier consistent with SDUHSD (Class AA), Steele Canyon Charter (10–17% of Class 3 Step 1), and Helix Charter (AD + Asst AD plus job-description supplement). Release time is already at 2 periods per year — the stipend dollar amount is the remaining gap.
5
Commission a board-requested peer-district athletics allocation survey
Formally request from business services a peer survey of (a) total athletics allocation as % of general fund, (b) officials funding split between district and ASB, (c) AT staffing model, across Sweetwater, SDUHSD, Poway, San Diego Unified, Oceanside, SMUSD, EUHSD, and the two GUHSD conversion charters. This closes the one remaining comparative gap in this brief and produces a defensible peer-anchored target for items 1–4.

The strategic frame

In a declining-enrollment environment where per-student LCFF revenue depends on ADA, and where two neighboring conversion charters (Helix, Steele Canyon) are compensating athletics staff at or above GUHSD's level, underfunded athletics is not only an equity question for current families — it is a retention question for future ADA. Each of the five recommendations above is within the district's existing fiscal capacity given $30–45M in reserves and the third-highest per-ADA spending in San Diego County.

Methodology

How this brief was compiled

Every numeric figure in this report is traceable to a primary-source document. No figures are estimated or inferred unless explicitly flagged as such.

Primary sources

  • GUHSD stipend data: Pulled from the district's Extended Curricular Assignments schedule, Board-approved 11/13/2025, with Coaching Stipends — Sports & Levels allocation exhibit updated 7/2023.
  • Granite Hills site-level spending: GUHSD Trust Account Statement reports and Arbiter Officiating Report, FY 2025-26 — provided by Granite Hills Athletics Administration. Covers 23 sport accounts, 182 Arbiter-assigned games, and 35 tournament entries.
  • Peer district stipend data: Board-approved salary schedules and collective bargaining agreements from Sweetwater UHSD (Appendix C-2, Board 1/6/2026), SDUHSD (Extra-Curricular Schedule, Board 6/11/2025), EUHSD (2025–26 ESTA, revised March 2026), SMUSD (SMEA/SMUSD CBA 2024–26 Section 8(D)), Helix Charter (2024–25 HTA Stipend List + 2025–26 Salary Schedule Board-approved 10/20/2025), and Steele Canyon Charter (SCCEA Contract 2022–26).
  • Athletic trainer compensation: Directly verified via current EdJoin job postings for SDUHSD (classified, BOC required) and Oceanside USD (classified, NATA required).
  • District budget data: California Department of Education Current Expense of Education per ADA file, FY 2023–24 (audited actuals).
  • Enrollment, reserves, and layoff context: Voice of San Diego reporting, March 2025.
  • Athletic trainer regulatory and staffing context: California AB 796 bill text; Pickett et al., Factors Influencing Athletic Training Services in California Secondary Schools: A 5-Year Update, Journal of Athletic Training, 2024.

Data gaps explicitly acknowledged

  • California school districts do not publish athletics as a standalone line in SACS financial reports. A true "athletics as % of general fund" figure for any district requires internal cost-center drill-down.
  • San Diego Unified's coaching stipend appendix and Poway USD's athletic stipend exhibit were not publicly available during research. SMUSD's coaching stipend class structure was verified but dollar amounts per class were not publicly isolatable.
  • Peer-district officials funding splits (what % each district covers from general fund vs. ASB) were not aggregated in any public source and are the subject of Recommendation #5.
  • Figures on peer-district AT staffing for Sweetwater, EUHSD, SMUSD, and Steele Canyon Charter are inferred from the absence of AT on their certificated stipend schedules — a strong inference given SDUHSD and Oceanside's verified classified model, but subject to confirmation.
