Anticipated Board Questions & Data-Anchored Responses

For use by GUHSD athletics administration preparing to present the Athletics Funding Case to the Governing Board. Every response is anchored to a primary source cited in the full report.

How to use this document: Scan it before the board meeting. If a trustee raises an objection during public comment or discussion, you'll recognize the pattern and have the exact data-anchored response ready. Do not read these verbatim — use them as confidence anchors. Every figure traces to the main report at jdavis-netizen.github.io/guhsd-athletics-funding-report.

The "we can't afford it" family

"We just laid off 61 people in February. How can we possibly afford $1.4M in new athletics spending?"

The district maintains $30M–$45M in general fund reserves — four times the state-mandated minimum (per Voice of San Diego, March 2025). The February 2025 layoffs were attributed to a $2.5M deficit. The full athletics proposal is $1.4M — roughly 56% of that same deficit figure, and less than 5% of reserves.

The layoff decision and the athletics funding question are separate allocation decisions, both within the district's existing fiscal capacity. Funding the full athletics proposal in year one would still leave GUHSD with reserves well above state mandate.

"Reserves exist for emergencies. We shouldn't spend them on stipends."

The proposal is primarily ongoing spending, not a reserve drawdown. The $1.4M represents recurring annual cost, which translates to 0.44% of the $321M general fund. Reserves are cited here as context for fiscal capacity — not as the funding source. A 0.44% annual allocation against the GF is a sustainable ongoing line.

Separately, the state mandate for reserve levels at a district of GUHSD's size is roughly 3% of total expenditures. GUHSD is running at roughly 4×. By the district's own reserve policy, there is meaningful headroom for strategic investments above the mandate floor without compromising the reserve's emergency purpose.

"Why not wait until the budget improves?"

Three reasons, each anchored in the data:

1. The gap is actively widening. CIF officials rates rose again in 2025-26. Helix's coaching stipend schedule adds 3 percentage points across the board in 2026-27 (head varsity coaches jumping from $9,251 to $11,664 at top tier). Every year GUHSD delays, the gap to peer districts grows.

2. ADA retention math doesn't wait. The two GUHSD conversion charters (Helix, Steele Canyon) collectively enroll 4,700+ students — 32% the size of GUHSD's ADA. Each student who transfers out costs the district ~$12K–$14K in LCFF revenue. Athletics compensation is one of the documentable structural advantages the charters use to attract and retain coaching staff, and by extension students.

3. Federal Title IX exposure doesn't wait. The OCR Morgan Hill USD resolution (October 2023) explicitly names athletic trainer access as a booster-disparity issue the district is required to remedy regardless of funding source.

The "but booster fundraising is how it works" family

"Athletics has always been booster-funded. Why is that a problem now?"

Two things have changed:

Federal Title IX enforcement posture has tightened. The October 2023 OCR resolution with Morgan Hill USD (Live Oak HS) specifically found that booster-driven disparities do not relieve the district of its Title IX obligation. Live Oak's booster model was described by a coach as "the wild west." The resolution required the district to create committees to offset booster-driven inequities and to ensure equitable access to the athletic trainer.

At Granite Hills in FY 2025-26 (verified trust account data): Boys sports spent $194,101 (55.3%); Girls sports spent $127,206 (36.2%). That $66,895 disparity is largely driven by differential booster-fundraising capacity by sport. Under Morgan Hill precedent, that disparity is GUHSD's obligation to remedy.

The "it's always been this way" defense is exactly what OCR explicitly rejected in the Morgan Hill resolution.

"Parents and boosters want to support athletics. That's a positive thing."

Agreed — and nothing in this proposal reduces what boosters can contribute. Boosters fund enhancements (team meals, travel, equipment beyond district standards). What this proposal addresses is the baseline operational costs — officials, assistant coach stipends, athletic trainer staffing — that are currently being covered by booster funds at GUHSD but are general-fund obligations at peer districts.

Peer districts like SDUHSD (spending 28% less per ADA than GUHSD) still fund the baseline — full-time classified AT at every comp site, 60+ coaching stipend positions, district-funded officials allocation closer to actual cost. Boosters at those districts layer on top of a fully-funded district baseline, not beneath one.

The "comparison isn't fair" family

"Helix is a charter, so it's not a fair comparison."

Three points on this:

Helix is a GUHSD conversion charter. It was part of GUHSD until 1998 — same footprint, same East County community, same sports league (Grossmont League). It is a direct comparison, not a distant one.

The pattern repeats at Steele Canyon Charter, which converted in 2007. Both charters fund ~40% more coaching stipend positions (72 and 73 vs. GUHSD's 52) and pay materially higher per-stipend rates at every tier. This is not one outlier — it's consistent across both conversion charters.

And the pattern holds across non-charter SD County peers too. SDUHSD and Oceanside USD — both traditional public school districts — treat the Athletic Trainer as full-time classified staff with benefits. SDUHSD pays its AD at the top of its schedule ($7,354). 6 of 7 SD County peers treat AT as classified. The charter vs. non-charter distinction doesn't explain away the consistent gap.

"Charters get more per-student funding than traditional public schools. Of course they spend more."

Actually, the opposite is true on the critical comparison. From verified CDE current expense of education data (FY 2023-24): GUHSD spends $21,644 per ADA — 3rd highest in the San Diego County peer set. Helix doesn't spend significantly more per student than GUHSD does.

The spending gap on athletics ($693K at Helix vs. ~$349K at GUHSD per school) isn't about Helix having more total resources. It's about how Helix allocates its available resources. Both districts operate in comparable revenue environments; Helix chooses to invest more of that in athletic stipends.

The "priorities" family

"Teacher salaries should come first. Why are we talking about coaches?"

Nearly all GUHSD athletic stipends are paid to teachers. Coaching stipends are extra-duty compensation on top of primary teaching salaries. Improving coaching stipends materially raises total compensation for the teachers who step up to coach — which is a retention tool for teachers, not a competitor to teacher salaries.

A classroom teacher who also serves as Head Varsity Football coach at GUHSD earns their base teaching salary plus $4,843 (at 1-3 years) to $7,611 (at 10+ years). At Helix, the same teacher earns base + $6,033 to $9,251. A $1,600-$2,500 annual stipend differential is a teacher-retention issue.

"What does this do for academics? Shouldn't we prioritize the core educational mission?"

Athletics is core educational mission in California. CIF participation rose to 852,575 student-athletes statewide in 2024-25 — an all-time high, up 11.6% over two years. In a declining-enrollment environment where total CA enrollment is down ~1.3%/year, athletics is one of the few school programs whose participation is growing.

For GUHSD specifically: the two conversion charters (Helix, Steele Canyon) compete directly for students from the same East County communities. Athletics is one of the documented reasons families choose those sites. When GUHSD underfunds athletics relative to those charters, students follow the investment — and each student lost = ~$12K–$14K in LCFF revenue to the district that otherwise could fund the academic core.

"Why not focus on academics first and deal with athletics later?"

Because the ADA retention impact of athletics investment funds academics. Every student retained in a GUHSD comprehensive program vs. a charter preserves roughly $12K–$14K per year in LCFF revenue. The $1.4M proposal is equivalent in order of magnitude to retaining roughly 100–115 students. Over a four-year high school enrollment cycle, that's potentially $5M–$6M in preserved LCFF revenue.

This isn't athletics vs. academics. It's strategic investment in the enrollment base that funds academics.

The "current system is fine" family

"The $22,500 athletic trainer stipend has worked for years. Why change it?"

Three reasons the current model doesn't meet today's standards:

1. It cannot recruit or retain a BOC-certified athletic trainer as a healthcare professional. A full-time certified AT in California earns $55K–$80K. A $22,500 stipend assumes the AT role is moonlighting duty added to another district job. SDUHSD and Oceanside USD — both directly verified — employ AT as full-time classified staff at $39K–$66K plus benefits, with BOC/NATA certification required.

2. The district already acknowledges the problem. GUHSD's own internal Athletic Trainer Staffing and Financial Realignment Proposal states the current bifurcated model (5 CTE teacher/AT sites + 4 walk-on sites) "does not ensure certified safety coverage at every campus." The $765K hybrid proposal was developed internally, not by outside advocates.

3. It creates measurable safety risk. Pierpoint et al. (Injury Epidemiology, 2018): schools without AT have 1.73× the overall girls' soccer injury rate and 6× the recurrent injury rate compared to schools with certified ATs. This applies directly to GUHSD's 4 walk-on sites (El Cajon Valley, Mount Miguel, El Capitan, Santana).

"Coaches have always worked for stipends that weren't full compensation. That's the nature of coaching."

GUHSD is not arguing coaches should be paid a full salary for coaching. The argument is that GUHSD's stipend amounts, slot counts, and AD placement are materially below peer districts in the same league and region. Specifically:

The "process" family

"Why hasn't this come through normal budget channels?"

The internal Athletic Trainer Staffing and Financial Realignment Proposal has been developed through athletics department channels. Tonight's presentation is intended to move the discussion to the Board because the scale of the decision ($1.4M ongoing, or $765K for just the AT piece) is above site-level authority and warrants Board policy direction.

The full proposal is available in writing. The Board can direct staff to return with an implementation plan, budget adjustment, or phased approach consistent with the Board's priorities.

"We need more data before we can make a decision."

Every claim in the presentation traces to a primary source. The data is not incomplete — it's more thoroughly verified than most board-level proposals. What the Board can reasonably request is an actual district-internal athletics cost-center pull from GUHSD Business Services to convert the rough bottom-up estimate ($3.1M district-wide) to a verified total.

But the headline findings — per-ADA spending, the peer AT comparison, the 52 vs. 72 vs. 73 stipend count gap, the $22,000 vs. $57,393 officials gap at Granite Hills — all rest on verified primary-source documents that Business Services cannot dispute.

A request for additional data is reasonable. A decision to defer action indefinitely pending that data, given the Title IX exposure and the 2026-27 growing-gap trajectory at Helix, is not neutral — it's a decision to continue the current allocation for another year.

The "narrow vs. broad" ask

"Even if we agree with some of this, $1.4M is a lot to approve at once."

Athletics leadership's recommended first-action ask is the Athletic Trainer restructuring alone: $765,000 ongoing + $12,670 one-time. That's 0.24% of the general fund — a single-digit percentage of reserves.

It's the strongest single lane of the full proposal (safety + Title IX + peer parity + district-internal proposal), and it achieves 100% certified AT coverage at every comprehensive site. The remaining items (coaching stipend equity, AD reclassification, officials allocation increase) can be addressed in subsequent Board actions as the AT implementation proceeds.

This is a staged approach that reduces implementation risk and allows the Board to evaluate the proposal's operational impact before committing to the full package.
