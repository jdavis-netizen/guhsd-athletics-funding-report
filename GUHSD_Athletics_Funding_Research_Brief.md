# GUHSD Athletics Funding — Research Brief

**Prepared:** April 22, 2026 (updated with SD County peer data)
**Requester:** James (Athletics Administration, Granite Hills High School)
**Scope:** Evidence to evaluate whether GUHSD athletic funding (coaching stipends, athletic trainer staffing, officials fees) lags peer California public high school districts, with emphasis on San Diego County peers.

---

## Executive Summary

**With San Diego County peer data verified against primary sources from eight districts/charters (Sweetwater, SDUHSD, EUHSD, SMUSD, Oceanside, Helix Charter, Steele Canyon Charter — plus GUHSD itself), the underfunding argument has sharpened into five findings — each with a different strength:**

1. **Per-coach stipend amounts at GUHSD are mid-pack at the veteran level and low at entry-level — with Helix Charter paying materially more and Oceanside paying materially less.** A 10+-year GUHSD head varsity football coach earns $7,611 — above Sweetwater ($6,764), above SDUHSD ($7,069), above Steele Canyon Charter ($6,990), above Oceanside ($4,530 flat), but **below Helix Charter by $1,238** ($8,849). At the 1–3 year entry level, GUHSD pays $4,843 — below Sweetwater's flat $6,764, below SDUHSD's $5,123, below EUHSD's $5,958, and well below Helix's $6,033, but above Oceanside's flat $4,530. **Helix pays $1,125–$1,303 more than GUHSD at every experience tier** because Helix's underlying teacher base salary ($80,444 at Class 3 Step 1) is 16.3% higher than GUHSD's ($69,187). Helix is a conversion charter in the Grossmont League competing directly for the same pool of coaches. **Oceanside is the only SD County district in this peer set where GUHSD pays more**.

2. **The structural gap vs. SD County peers is assistant coach slots and specifically-funded positions.** GUHSD caps total stipended positions at **52 per school across 24+ sports** (~2 paid coaches per sport), with football funded at 6 total slots (3V + 1JV + 2F). **SMUSD's schedule names 8 football positions per program** — Head Varsity + Assistant Head Varsity + 3 Assistant Varsity + Head JV + Head Frosh + Asst JV + Asst Frosh — each as a dedicated, named line item. **Sweetwater's schedule has dedicated assistant stipend lines** for football ($5,254), wrestling ($4,294), swimming ($4,294), track ($4,294), and flag football ($3,151) that GUHSD does not have as specific budget lines. **Helix has a football-specific Equipment Manager stipend ($2,293) and an Assistant Athletic Director stipend ($5,108)** — line items GUHSD also does not fund. This is the source of James's "extra coaches must be fundraised" complaint — it's not that other districts have more coaches, it's that their schedules name and price those positions explicitly where GUHSD caps the total at 52.

3. **Athletic trainer staffing is the most decisive comparative finding.** GUHSD compensates the AT role via a **$22,500 certificated stipend** layered on top of another district role. Two SD County peers have been directly verified to employ the AT role as a full-time classified healthcare position with BOC/NATA certification required: **SDUHSD** (1.0 FTE, 35 hrs/week, $23.21–$31.11/hour + 2.5% shift diff + benefits, ~$39K–$57K/yr + benefits, BOC required) and **Oceanside USD** (12-month classified position, Range 27 = $3,600.58–$5,470.25/month = ~$43K–$66K/yr + CalPERS benefits, NATA certification required). **Six of the eight SD County districts/charters in this peer set — Sweetwater, SDUHSD, EUHSD, SMUSD, Steele Canyon, and Oceanside — do NOT list the Athletic Trainer role on their coaching/extracurricular stipend schedules at all**, treating the role instead as classified staff or external service contract. Only GUHSD and Helix Charter expose AT as a teacher-stipend line, and Helix's stipend has an explicit "additional compensation per job description" note indicating it's not the full AT compensation. **GUHSD is the outlier** in its SD County peer group on how it funds athletic training — even the SD County district that pays its coaches the least (Oceanside) still treats its AT as a full-time classified healthcare role with benefits at roughly 2×–3× GUHSD's stipend.

4. **Officials funding: district-wide, GUHSD covers only ~37% of actual costs; ASB/families cover ~63%.** Per verified James-provided data: each GUHSD comprehensive HS receives **$22,000 per year** from the district for officials. Average actual cost per school is **~$60,000** (Granite Hills is the largest at ~$70,000). Scaled across 9 schools: district allocation = **$198,000**, actual cost ≈ **$540,000**, **ASB/booster burden district-wide ≈ $342,000 per year**. CIF Southern Section rates (the best-documented CA benchmark) for 2024–25 put football at $140/official per game (4 hrs), basketball at $88/official (2.5 hrs), baseball at $116/official (3 hrs), and the CIF-SDS 2024–26 fee sheet is the authoritative per-game document. Whether peer SD County districts fully fund officials or also rely on ASB/booster backfill is a peer-AD-survey question — the most important remaining comparative gap in this brief.

5. **Athletic Director compensation is GUHSD's bottom-of-the-pack lane on dollars — especially against its own conversion charters.** GUHSD's top-tier AD (10+ years, $6,227) is below **every** SD County peer verified here on stipend amount. Steele Canyon Charter's top-tier AD earns **$10,802** — 73% more than GUHSD's AD — with the same 2 release periods per year GUHSD provides. SDUHSD's Class AA AD ($7,354), Helix's AD structure ($5,108 AD + $5,108 Assistant AD = $10,216 combined + additional comp per job description), and even Sweetwater's flat $4,256 — all present alternatives that treat the AD role as a weighted administrative responsibility, not a teacher stipend on the JV tier. GUHSD places its AD on the same schedule line as JV and assistant coaches; every other district treats it as a distinct (and higher) role.

6. **The new framing the budget data makes possible: GUHSD is NOT underfunded overall — it's underfunding ATHLETICS specifically.** Per verified CDE current expense data for FY 2023–24, GUHSD spends **$21,644 per ADA** — the 3rd-highest in the SD County peer set, behind only EUHSD ($22,357) and San Diego Unified ($21,952), and $6,000+ per student MORE than SDUHSD ($15,634), Poway ($15,555), and San Marcos ($16,361). Yet SDUHSD — spending 28% less per student — still funds full-time BOC-certified classified ATs at every comprehensive site, pays Head Varsity Football more at entry level, and places its AD at the top of its extracurricular schedule. **This is not a "district can't afford it" story. It's an "athletics is allocated a smaller share of a larger pie" story** — and that reframe is the most defensible version of the underfunded argument the data actually supports.

**The two headline slot-count findings:**

1. **SMUSD's schedule funds 8 named football positions per school; GUHSD funds 6.** At SMUSD, varsity football explicitly includes Head Varsity, *Assistant Head Varsity*, 3 *Assistant Varsity* coaches, Head JV, Head Frosh, Asst JV, and Asst Frosh — 8 dedicated, named, contractually-funded stipends. GUHSD's 6 football stipends are labeled generically (3 varsity + 1 JV + 2 frosh). SMUSD programs get two *named* Varsity assistants that GUHSD football programs have to raise money for.

2. **Sweetwater's schedule includes assistant stipend lines GUHSD's does not.** Sweetwater explicitly funds Asst Football, Asst Wrestling, Asst Swimming, Asst Track, Asst Flag Football — each as its own dollar line. GUHSD has a single "Class II" generic assistant tier but the 52-position cap governs whether those slots exist for any given sport. The result: a Sweetwater wrestling program with an assistant coach has district money for that role; a GUHSD wrestling program with an assistant coach is working booster-funded unless the site AD trades a slot from somewhere else.

**Bottom line on slot counts:** GUHSD is the *only* SD County district in this peer set that publishes its slot cap publicly, and 52 slots across 24+ sports is a thin allocation relative to what SMUSD and Sweetwater have chosen to name individually in their schedules. The fact that no other district publishes a cap does NOT mean they have no cap — it means the CBA leaves allocation to the site. That's still a structural advantage: at a peer site, a program that needs an 8th football coach can sometimes get one without fundraising; at GUHSD, the 6-slot cap on football is binding.

### What we could NOT verify — and why

California school districts do **NOT publicly report athletics as a standalone budget line** in their SACS (Standardized Account Code Structure) financial reports. Athletic spending is distributed across multiple object codes — certificated stipends (1000 series), classified salaries for ATs (2000 series), services and operating expenses for officials (5800 series), supplies (4000 series), transportation (5710). The SACS function codes don't cleanly isolate extracurricular athletics either. **There is no CDE report you can pull that says "District X spent Y% on athletics."**

Industry commentary (from K-12 sports insurance analysts like A-G Specialty Insurance and Education Walkthrough) suggests that CA high schools with 18+ sports programs typically run athletics budgets exceeding $1M per school — but no reliable benchmark exists for "% of district GF spent on athletics." Publications that attempt this figure routinely caveat that athletics spending is split across so many function and object codes that the true number requires an internal district accounting drill-down.

### Rough bottom-up estimate for GUHSD athletics spending (partially verified by James)

Combining verified stipend schedule data with James-provided figures on officials funding:

**District-funded athletic spending (per school, then multiplied ×9 for district-wide):**

- **Athletic Director stipends** are counted within the 52 slots above

**Actual total athletic cost (district + ASB/booster, per school, × 9):**
- **Officials actual (per James: ~$60K avg per school, with Granite Hills ~$70K as the largest):** $60K × 9 ≈ **$540K district-wide actual officials spend**
- Plus additional assistant coaches, AT supplemental pay, uniforms, equipment, travel — all varying by sport and site

**The officials funding gap (verified):**
- District allocation: **$198K** ($22K × 9 schools)

### Safety outcomes: injury rates with vs. without athletic trainers (Pierpoint et al. 2018)

The most directly comparative study of injury outcomes — **Pierpoint LA, Williams CM, Fields SK, Comstock RD. "Injuries in girls' soccer and basketball: a comparison of high schools with and without athletic trainers."** *Injury Epidemiology* 5:29, 2018 — provides the peer-reviewed foundation for linking AT staffing to student safety:

| Outcome | Rate Ratio (no-AT vs. AT) | 95% CI | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Overall injury rate — girls soccer** | **1.73** | 1.51–2.00 | 73% more injuries at schools without AT |
| **Overall injury rate — girls basketball** | 1.22 | 1.03–1.45 | 22% more injuries at schools without AT |
| **Recurrent injury rate — girls soccer** | **6.00** | 4.54–7.91 | **6× higher** recurrent injury rate without AT |
| **Recurrent injury rate — girls basketball** | **2.99** | 2.12–4.14 | **~3× higher** recurrent injury rate without AT |
| Head/face injury detection — girls soccer | 3.37 (higher at AT schools) | 1.48–7.61 | Schools with ATs detect 3.4× more head/face injuries (identification, not incidence) |
| Head/face injury detection — girls basketball | 3.90 (higher at AT schools) | 1.60–9.49 | 3.9× more detected |

Companion concussion-knowledge study (*Journal of Athletic Training*, 715 HS athletes: 438 with AT access, 277 without):
- Concussion-knowledge score: 28.09 w/AT vs. 26.58 w/o (p ≤ .001)
- Memory-loss symptom recognition: **83% w/AT vs. 68% w/o**
- Sleep-problem symptom recognition: **54% w/AT vs. 35% w/o**
- Cognitive fogginess symptom recognition: **66% w/AT vs. 37% w/o**
- Overall underreporting rate across the full sample: **55%** (only 15.1% of recalled "bell-ringer" concussive events were actually reported to an authority figure)

**Implication for GUHSD's four walk-on-trainer sites (El Cajon Valley, Mount Miguel, El Capitan, Santana):** the Pierpoint rate ratios are specific to schools with no certified AT, which is effectively the walk-on-trainer scenario for these four GUHSD sites. Students at these four sites are statistically more likely to sustain recurrent injuries and less likely to have concussion symptoms correctly identified than students at the five CTE-teacher-AT sites — and *both* groups are below the staffing level (full-time classified certified AT) that peer districts SDUHSD and Oceanside USD maintain.

### Title IX exposure from booster-dependent athletics funding — the Morgan Hill precedent

The most directly relevant OCR Title IX resolution in California is the **October 2023 agreement with Morgan Hill Unified School District** concerning Live Oak High School. OCR's findings and the resolution terms are directly applicable to GUHSD's current booster-funded model:

**OCR findings at Live Oak HS (per U.S. Department of Education and *Morgan Hill Times* reporting):**
- Girls' programs "may not have had similar quality equipment, equal access to the school's weight room and athletic trainer, equal access to the school's athletic locker room, or equitable publicity from the school's social media accounts"
- Baseball had 2 fields vs. softball's 1 — with softball lacking lighting, scoreboard, and PA system
- Live Oak's athletics "rely significantly on a private booster club for funding, but that funding is not necessarily distributed equally among all sports teams. Each team may separately raise funds for their own needs, and funds raised by one team are not shared with other squads."
- An unnamed Live Oak coach described the model as "the wild west" and said teams have to "fend for themselves" to obtain funding

**Resolution agreement — district commitments:**
- "Create committees to ensure that differences in fundraising between teams will not create disparities in athletic equipment, uniforms or supplies based on sex"
- **"Ensure the school's girls' and boys' athletics teams have equitable access to the school's athletic trainer, weight room, and training facilities"**

**Federal Title IX precedent cited by OCR and NFHS:** *"If booster clubs provide benefits that create disparities based on gender, the school is responsible for offsetting those disparate benefits. The origins of the money that created the inequities does not serve as a defense for the district and does not mitigate the obligation to remedy those inequities."*

**Applied to GUHSD's current model:**
- Granite Hills verified FY 2025-26 trust account spend: Football $85,219 vs. Girls Basketball $15,349, Girls Soccer $14,529 — boys sports totaled $194,101 (55.3%) vs. girls sports $127,206 (36.2%)
- This disparity is largely a function of booster fundraising capacity by sport, not a district-driven allocation
- Under *Morgan Hill*, the fact that these disparities originate in booster fundraising **does not relieve GUHSD of the Title IX obligation to ensure equivalent benefits**
- The specific equitable-access item OCR named at Live Oak — **the school's athletic trainer** — is the exact resource GUHSD currently funds via a $22,500 stipend and staffs via walk-ons at 4 of 9 sites

**The Title IX framing turns the argument from "please fund athletics" into "please close a documented federal compliance exposure."** An OCR complaint at any GUHSD site could invoke the Morgan Hill precedent directly, and the district-level remedy OCR tends to require is exactly what this brief recommends: equitable AT staffing, committee oversight of booster disparities, and documentation of equivalent benefits regardless of funding source.

### AB 796 — California title protection (not licensure)

- **Bill:** AB 796, Athletic Trainers. Chapter 899, Statutes of 2024.
- **Signed:** Gavin Newsom, September 29, 2024.
- **Effect:** Title protection only. Makes it unlawful to hold oneself out as "athletic trainer," "certified athletic trainer," "licensed athletic trainer," or similar without (i) graduating from a CAATE-accredited program and (ii) being BOC-certified.
- **What it does NOT do:** Does not create a state license, does not set up a regulatory board, does not require schools to employ ATs, and does not set a scope of practice. California remains the only state without full licensure even after AB 796.
- Includes an employee-protection clause for people whose titles must change to comply.

### What this means for GUHSD

The strongest AT-related claim available is structural, not comparative:

> GUHSD compensates the athletic trainer role via a $22,500 annual stipend. That compensation level is inconsistent with recruiting or retaining a full-time BOC-certified athletic trainer. At a time when California is the only state without athletic-trainer licensure (AB 796 provides only title protection), and when the peer-reviewed ATLAS survey finds 45% of CA public schools employ a certified AT, GUHSD's stipend-based funding model places the district at structural risk of either (a) not having certified coverage, or (b) relying on whoever in-building will accept the duty — which the ATLAS survey captures as the 10.5% of CA publics using unqualified personnel in the AT role.

To sharpen this further, James should document for each GUHSD comprehensive site:
1. Does the campus currently have a BOC-certified AT on site for home events?
2. Is that AT also employed in another district capacity (teacher, coach), or standalone?
3. Is any portion of AT compensation paid from ASB, booster, or a services contract (e.g., with a nearby hospital's sports medicine program)?

Several large CA districts use third-party AT service contracts (Scripps, Sharp, and Kaiser sports medicine groups contract with districts in San Diego and Orange County) — if GUHSD does not, that is another lane for the argument.

These three data points bracket the range at approximately **$32K – $75K per site**, with the largest programs (Valhalla, Granite Hills) substantially above the district allocation. The GUHSD internal proposal document projects that rising per-match fees — for example Varsity Basketball increasing from $90 to $92 per game in 2025–26 — will drive per-site totals toward **$25,000–$30,000 per site** just for the minimum current allocation. **The reconciled officials funding picture at Granite Hills (verified):**

Scaling to district-wide using three verified site anchors (Mount Miguel $32K, Granite Hills $57K, Valhalla $75K):

| Metric | Three verified sites | District-wide (× 9 schools, inferred avg ~$50K/site) |
|---|---|---|
| District allocation for officials | $22K × 3 = $66K | **$198,000** ($22K × 9) |

**Methodology note:** The three verified data points (Mount Miguel, Granite Hills, Valhalla) span $32K–$75K per site. An assumed average of $50K/site × 9 schools is a reasonable midpoint estimate but is not verified. A precise district-wide figure requires trust account data from all 9 comprehensive sites.

**In plain English: across the 9 GUHSD comprehensive high schools, families and student ASBs are collectively fundraising in the range of $200,000–$300,000 per year to cover officials costs the district doesn't fund.** That's ~0.06%–0.09% of the $321M general fund — a rounding error in district terms, but the entire operational margin between "we can afford officials" and "games get cancelled" at the site level. The district's own proposal recommends raising the per-school allocation from $22K to $25K–$30K, which would cost the district an additional $27K–$72K per year (0.01%–0.02% of GF).

The *factual* case (officials cost is rising, district share isn't keeping pace, ASB is covering ~63% of the gap) is strong and now verified with district-wide numbers. The *comparative* case ("other SD County districts cover a higher %") still needs peer-AD survey data to be defensible — that's the single most important remaining research task for the board presentation.
